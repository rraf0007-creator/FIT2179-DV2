Who Studies in Australia?

International student enrolments in Australia are concentrated in a few major source countries, a few large states, and a rapidly changing post-pandemic migration landscape.

565,601 January 2026 year-to-date enrolments
129,164 enrolments from China
NSW + VIC largest study destinations

Overview

Australia’s international student population

Australia recorded 565,601 international student enrolments in January 2026, but the numbers are not evenly spread. This visualisation follows three connected questions: where students come from, where and what they study in Australia, and how student migration changed after the border closure period. By combining enrolment data with ABS migration data, the story moves from education demand to the broader movement of students across Australia’s border.

Chapter 1

Who comes to Australia?

Australia’s international student population is strongly concentrated. A small number of countries account for a large share of enrolments, meaning that changes in only a few major source countries can strongly affect the scale and direction of Australia’s international education sector.

China, India and Nepal lead Australia’s student source countries

Which countries send the most international students to Australia?

This ranking shows that Australia’s international student population is not evenly spread across source countries. China remains the largest source country, followed by India and Nepal, creating a clear top-three pattern. Bangladesh appearing in the top five is also important because it shows that the South Asian student market extends beyond India and Nepal. The chart therefore introduces one of the main themes of the page: Australia’s international education sector is heavily connected to Asia, especially East and South Asia. This matters because policy changes, economic conditions, exchange rates, or visa settings affecting these countries can influence Australia’s overall international student numbers.

Where do students from major source countries enrol?

Which states do students coming from different parts of the world prefer? This flow diagram connects the top five source countries, plus all other countries grouped together, to the Australian states and territories where those enrolments are recorded.

The flow diagram moves one step beyond the country ranking by showing where students from major source countries enrol inside Australia. Thicker flows represent stronger source-country-to-state connections. China and India send large numbers of students to both New South Wales and Victoria, which helps explain why those two states dominate later maps. Nepal is especially concentrated in New South Wales, suggesting that different student communities do not distribute evenly across the country. The “Other countries” group is also important: even though the top five countries are very large, Australia’s student population still includes a wide range of smaller source countries. This makes the system both concentrated and diverse at the same time.

Which continents and countries make up the largest share?

Asia accounts for the overwhelming majority of Australia’s international students, so it is expanded into its largest source countries. The remaining regions are grouped in a separate panel on the right with each of their respective percentages.

The treemap adds hierarchy to the source-country story. Instead of treating every country as a separate equal category, it first shows the continent-level imbalance, then expands Asia into its major source countries. Asia receives most of the visual space because it contributes most enrolments, while the smaller regions are grouped together on the right to keep the chart readable. Within Asia, the largest tiles show that China and India alone account for a substantial share of all international student enrolments, while Nepal and the “Other Asia” group show that the rest of the Asian market is also significant. This chart helps the viewer understand why Asia is not just one category in the dataset, but the main geographic foundation of Australia’s international student population.

Chapter 2

Which sector and states do they prefer?

After looking at where students come from, the next step is to examine what they study and where they enrol within Australia. Enrolments are concentrated in the largest study destinations, especially New South Wales and Victoria, while sector data shows that Higher Education remains the dominant part of international education.

Higher Education leads, while Vocational Education and Training has grown after the rebound

Higher Education remains the largest sector, while Vocational Education and Training has become a much larger part of international enrolments after the post-pandemic rebound.

This stacked area chart shows that Higher Education remains the dominant sector, while Vocational Education and Training became more visible after the post-pandemic rebound. The dip during the border closure period highlights how strongly international education depends on student mobility, and the recovery after borders reopened shows that demand returned quickly.

Higher Education dominates the January 2026 sector mix

In January 2026 year-to-date data, Higher Education dominates international student enrolments, followed by the Vocational Education and Training (VET) sector.

This donut chart shows that Higher Education makes up the largest share of January 2026 enrolments, followed by Vocational Education and Training (VET). Smaller sectors such as Schools, ELICOS and Non-award study are present but much smaller, making tertiary education the main driver of Australia’s international student sector.

Where are international students most concentrated relative to state population?

This map shows how international student enrolments are distributed across Australian states and territories.

This choropleth map shows net student migration relative to each state’s population, using net student migration per 100,000 residents. This changes the question from “which states gained the most student migrants in total?” to “where was student migration most intense relative to the size of the local population?”. The most striking moment is 2020–21, when the map turns blue across most states. This reflects the COVID-19 border closure period: very few new international students could enter Australia, while many existing students left, so departures were higher than arrivals. After borders reopened, the map shifts back toward warmer colours, showing the recovery in student arrivals from 2022–23 onwards. The tooltip still shows the raw arrivals, departures and net migration values, so the viewer can compare both total scale and population-adjusted intensity.

Chapter 3

Enrolment shows study location; migration shows movement

Enrolments show where students are studying, but migration data shows how students move in and out of Australia. The ABS data adds a wider population perspective by tracking student visa arrivals, departures and net migration.

Enrolments fell during border closures and rebounded after 2022

The time series shows how international student enrolments changed before, during and after the border closure period.

The line chart shows the disruption caused by the border closure period and the recovery that followed. Enrolments dropped sharply around the closure period and reached a low point in 2022, showing how dependent international education is on cross-border movement. From 2023 onwards, enrolments rebounded strongly, suggesting that demand for Australian study remained high once students could return. The 2026 value needs careful reading: it is January year-to-date data, not a complete annual total, so it should not be interpreted as a full-year decline. This chart acts as a bridge between the enrolment-focused sections and the migration-focused sections, because the enrolment pattern closely reflects changes in student movement.

Student migration collapsed during border closures, then rebounded

Student migration dropped sharply during the border closure period, then rebounded strongly after 2021–22. The black line shows net student migration: arrivals minus departures.

This chart explains the movement behind the enrolment trend. During the border closure period, student arrivals fell dramatically while departures remained high, which pushed net student migration down. This means Australia was losing more student migrants than it was gaining during that period. After borders reopened, arrivals recovered quickly and net student migration became strongly positive again. The black net migration line is especially important because it summarises the balance between people entering and leaving: a positive value means student arrivals exceeded departures, while a negative value means departures were greater. This helps show that international students are not only an education story, but also a major part of Australia’s population movement story.

Student visas were the largest selected temporary visa arrival group

This chart places student visas within the broader temporary visa migration picture for 2024–25.

The lollipop chart compares student visas with other selected temporary visa migrant arrival groups in 2024–25. Student visas appear as the largest group, which shows that international students are not a minor subset of temporary migration. They are one of the most important categories in the temporary migration system. Comparing student visas with working holiday, visitor and skilled temporary categories gives the education story a wider context: student movement is large enough to influence national migration patterns, not just university enrolment figures. This is why the project uses both Department of Education data and ABS migration data: the two datasets describe different parts of the same broader story.

Higher Education drives most student visa migrant arrivals

Student visa arrivals are not evenly split across education types.

This composition chart breaks student visa migrant arrivals into education categories. Higher Education makes up the largest share, which reinforces the central role of universities in both enrolment and migration patterns. Vocational Education and Training (VET) and other student visa categories contribute smaller shares, but they still matter because they show that student migration is not limited to one pathway. The chart also helps connect the migration data back to the sector charts earlier in the page: the same dominance of Higher Education appears in both the enrolment dataset and the ABS student visa arrivals dataset. This consistency strengthens the overall story that universities remain the main anchor of Australia’s international student system.

Chapter 4

Student migration is concentrated in the largest study states

The migration data also has a strong state pattern. The largest enrolment states are also the places gaining the largest numbers of student migrants, although the scale of migration varies over time.

How does student migration relate to student enrolment across the states?

This chart combines Department of Education enrolment data with ABS migration flow data to compare the size of international student enrolments with net student migration.

This chart compares two related but different measures: Department of Education enrolments and ABS net student migration. States with larger enrolment populations generally record larger student migration gains, especially New South Wales and Victoria. This shows that the biggest study destinations are also major points of student population movement, while the colour scale adds another layer by showing net migration relative to enrolment size.

Where is student migration most concentrated relative to population?

This interactive choropleth map shows net student migration per 100,000 residents by state and territory. Use the slider to compare financial years from 2004–05 to 2024–25.

This map normalises ABS net student migration by state population, making smaller states easier to compare with New South Wales and Victoria. The slider shows the sharp disruption around 2020–21, when student arrivals collapsed and net student migration became negative or much weaker in many places. The rebound after 2022–23 shows how quickly student migration recovered once international movement resumed. The tooltip shows arrivals, departures and net student migration, so the viewer can compare both the population-adjusted pattern and the raw migration values.

What this shows overall

1. Source countries are concentrated

A small group of countries contributes a large share of international student enrolments, especially China, India and Nepal. This concentration means that changes in a few major source countries can strongly affect the whole international education sector.

2. Study destinations are uneven

New South Wales and Victoria dominate international enrolments, while smaller states and territories attract much smaller student populations. The geographic pattern is therefore concentrated rather than evenly national.

3. Students shape migration flows

ABS migration data shows that student visa arrivals are a major part of Australia’s temporary migration system, especially after the post-pandemic rebound. Student data therefore helps explain both education and migration patterns.
