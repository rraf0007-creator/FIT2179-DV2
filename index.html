Who Studies in Australia?

International student enrolments in Australia are concentrated in a few major source countries, a few large states, and a rapidly changing post-pandemic migration landscape.

Overview

Australia’s international student population

Australia recorded 565,601 international student enrolments in January 2026, but the numbers are not evenly spread. Which countries send the most students? Which states attract them? What sectors do they study in, and how did student migration change after the border closures? This visualisation follows the story behind Australia’s international students through enrolment and migration data.

Chapter 1

Who comes to Australia?

Australia’s international student population is not evenly spread across the world. A small number of source countries account for a large share of enrolments, meaning changes in these countries can strongly affect Australia’s education sector.

Top source countries

Which countries send the most international students to Australia?

Key takeaway: China remains the largest source country for international student enrolments, followed by India and Nepal. Bangladesh also appears in the top five, showing the importance of South Asian countries in Australia’s international education system.

Enrolments over time

How have international student enrolments changed over time?

Key takeaway: Enrolments fell sharply around the border closure period, reached a low point in 2022, and then rebounded strongly from 2023 onwards. The 2026 value is January year-to-date data, not a complete full-year total.

Where do students from major source countries enrol?

This flow diagram connects the top five source countries, plus all other countries, to the Australian states and territories where those enrolments are recorded.

Key takeaway: China and India send large numbers of students to both New South Wales and Victoria, while Nepal is especially concentrated in New South Wales. The “Other countries” group shows that Australia’s international student population is broader than only the top five source countries.

Which countries make up the largest share?

International students come mostly from Asia, but each continent has a different source-country pattern. Each panel shows the largest source countries within that continent, while the panel heading shows that continent’s share of Australia’s international student population.

Chapter 2

What and where do they study?

The student population is also uneven inside Australia. Enrolments are concentrated in the largest study destinations, especially New South Wales and Victoria, while sector data shows that Higher Education remains the dominant part of international education.

How has the sector mix changed?

Higher Education remains the largest sector, while VET has become a much larger part of international enrolments after the post-pandemic rebound.

Key takeaway: Higher Education remains the largest sector across the period, while VET has become a much more visible part of the enrolment mix after the post-pandemic rebound.

Sector breakdown of enrolments, Jan 2026

In January 2026 YTD data, Higher Education dominates international student enrolments, followed by the VET sector.

Key takeaway: Higher Education accounts for the largest share of international enrolments, followed by VET. Smaller sectors such as Schools, ELICOS and Non-award study make up a much smaller share.

Where do international students study in Australia?

This map shows how international student enrolments are distributed across Australian states and territories.

Key takeaway: New South Wales and Victoria dominate the enrolment map. Queensland, Western Australia and South Australia form a second tier, while Tasmania and the Northern Territory have much smaller international student populations.

Chapter 3

Why migration data matters

Enrolments show where students are studying, but migration data shows how students move in and out of Australia. The ABS data adds a wider population perspective by tracking student visa arrivals, departures and net migration.

Student visa arrivals and departures over time

Student migration dropped sharply during the border closure period, then rebounded strongly after 2021–22. The black line shows net student migration: arrivals minus departures.

Key takeaway: Student migration collapsed during the border closure period, when departures were high and arrivals were extremely low. After borders reopened, arrivals rebounded quickly and net student migration became strongly positive again.

Temporary visa arrivals

This chart places student visas within the broader temporary visa migration picture.

Key takeaway: In 2024–25, student visas were the largest selected temporary visa migrant arrival group, showing that international students are also a major part of Australia’s temporary migration system.

What type of student visas drive migrant arrivals?

Student visa arrivals are not evenly split across education types.

Key takeaway: Most student visa migrant arrivals in 2024–25 were linked to Higher Education, reinforcing the central role of universities in Australia’s international student migration story.

Chapter 4

Where is student migration concentrated?

The migration data also has a strong state pattern. The largest enrolment states are also the places gaining the largest numbers of student migrants, although the scale of migration varies over time.

Enrolments vs net migration by state

This chart combines Department of Education enrolment data with ABS migration flow data to compare the size of international student enrolments with net student migration.

Key takeaway: States with larger enrolment populations also tend to record larger net student migration gains. New South Wales and Victoria stand out because they combine high enrolment numbers with high student migration flows.

How did student migration change across Australia?

This interactive choropleth map shows net student migration by state and territory. Use the slider to compare financial years from 2004–05 to 2024–25.

Key takeaway: The choropleth map shows how net student migration varies by state and financial year. The slider reveals the sharp negative impact of 2020–21 and the strong rebound from 2022–23 onwards.

Key takeaways

1. Source countries are concentrated

A small group of countries contributes a large share of international student enrolments, especially China, India and Nepal.

2. Study destinations are uneven

New South Wales and Victoria dominate international enrolments, while smaller states and territories attract much smaller student populations.

3. Students shape migration flows

ABS migration data shows that student visa arrivals are a major part of Australia’s temporary migration system, especially after the post-pandemic rebound.
